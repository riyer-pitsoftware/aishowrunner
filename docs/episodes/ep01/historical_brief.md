## 1. Manyakheta in 814 CE

### The City and Its Reputation

The Rashtrakuta capital was among the great cities of the early medieval world. The Arab geographer Al-Masudi (writing c. 943 CE, but drawing on earlier accounts including the merchant Sulaiman, c. 851 CE) counted the Rashtrakuta king among four greatest sovereigns on earth, alongside the Caliph of Baghdad, the Emperor of Byzantium, and the Emperor of China. By 814 CE, under Govinda III, the Rashtrakutas controlled territory from the Narmada to the Tungabhadra and projected power far beyond — an empire that had humiliated the Pratiharas and forced tribute from the Palas.

Manyakheta sat on the northern Deccan plateau, in the doab between the Bhima and Krishna rivers, in what is today Gulbarga district, Karnataka. The site was strategic: close enough to the northern frontiers to respond to Pratihara incursions, central enough to control the Deccan trade routes running east-west between the Konkan coast and the Bay of Bengal.

**Source note:** The identification of Manyakheta with modern Malkhed is well established from inscriptions and the Jain text *Vikramankadeva-charita*. Archaeological remains at Malkhed are sparse — the city was sacked repeatedly (by the Paramara king Siyaka II in 972 CE and others), and modern habitation overlays the old city. Much of the physical layout below is **[INFERRED]** from comparable Rashtrakuta-period sites, inscriptional evidence, and Arab travellers' accounts.

### Physical Layout

**[INFERRED from Rashtrakuta inscriptions, Arab accounts, and analogous capitals like Badami and Pattadakal]**

The city would have followed the general pattern of early medieval Deccan capitals:

- **Palace complex (rajabhavana):** The fortified royal enclosure, likely on elevated ground. Rashtrakuta palatial architecture would draw on the same vocabulary as Ellora and Pattadakal — monumental carved stone for prestige buildings, brick-and-mortar for functional structures. The palace compound would include the audience hall (*sabha-mandapa*), residential quarters, treasury, and administrative offices. Fortification walls of laterite and dressed stone, with gateways (*dvara*) at cardinal points.

- **Temple quarter:** Multiple religious establishments clustered near the palace but also scattered through the city. Hindu temples (both Shaiva and Vaishnava), Jain basadis, and likely at least one Buddhist *vihara*. The Rashtrakutas were ecumenical patrons — Govinda III's own inscriptions invoke Shiva, but his predecessors and successors patronized Jain institutions lavishly. Temples would be in the Dravida style characteristic of the western Deccan: stepped pyramidal *vimana*, pillared *mandapa*, carved exterior walls. Smaller shrines in brick.

- **Market streets (*hatta*):** Arab travellers describe Indian cities of this period as having organized bazaars. Manyakheta would have had textile markets (cotton from the black-soil hinterland was a major commodity), metalwork shops, oil-pressers, potters, and jewellers. The Jain merchant guilds (*sreni*) likely dominated the textile and long-distance trade. Markets would be along the main processional road connecting the palace gate to the principal temple.

- **Administrative quarter:** The *akshapataladhikari* (keeper of records) maintained the charter archives here. Scribes (*kayastha*) worked in purpose-built halls. Copper plates awaiting ratification would be stored in secured rooms.

- **Residential quarters:** Arranged loosely by occupation and community. Brahmin *agraharas* (tax-exempt settlements gifted by royal charter) would be on ritually clean ground near water sources. Merchant quarters near the markets. Artisan quarters organised by craft.

- **Water infrastructure:** Tanks (*tataka*), wells, and channels. The Deccan relies on monsoon-fed tanks, and a major city would have substantial hydraulic engineering. Stepped wells (*vavi*) for daily use. Ritual bathing tanks near temples.

- **Peripheral settlements:** Outside the main fortification walls: lower-caste occupational quarters, including the *chandala* and *matanga* settlements (see Section 5). Cremation grounds. Animal markets. Travellers' rest houses (*dharmasala*).

### Sensory World

**What Manyakheta looked like:**
Red-brown laterite and grey-black basalt — the signature stones of the Deccan. Whitewashed brick walls catching the morning light. Temple *shikhara* towers visible above the roofline. The palace walls would be plastered and possibly painted (Rashtrakuta-period painted plaster survives at Ellora caves). Streets of packed earth, turning to black mud during monsoon. Cotton awnings over market stalls. Cattle, goats, and elephants (royal stables) would be common sights.

**What it smelled like:**
Sandalwood paste and incense from temple worship drifting over the Brahmin quarter. Cow dung fuel burning in domestic hearths (and used for floor-sealing — freshly applied cow-dung wash has a sharp, clean scent). Jasmine and *champaka* flowers strung for temple offerings. In the market: sesame oil pressing, turmeric powder, raw cotton bales. Near the peripheral quarters: tanning leather, rendering animal fat, the acrid sweetness of cremation pyres. After monsoon rains: the overwhelming petrichor of water on black cotton soil.

**What it sounded like:**
Temple bells at dawn and dusk. The *nadaswaram* (double-reed instrument) and *mridangam* (drum) at temple rituals. Conch-shell blowing to mark auspicious hours. Street vendors calling in Kannada (the court language of record) with Sanskrit heard primarily in ritual contexts. The clang of metalworkers. Bullock-cart wheels on packed earth. In the palace: the rhythmic chant of Vedic recitation from the *rajapurohita*'s daily rituals.

### Climate and Geography

Manyakheta sits on the northern Karnataka plateau at roughly 400–500 metres elevation. The landscape is characteristic Deccan: gently undulating terrain of black cotton soil (*regur*) over basalt bedrock, with scrubby vegetation, scattered neem and banyan trees, and seasonal watercourses.

- **Pre-monsoon (March–June):** Brutal dry heat, temperatures reaching 40°C+. Dust storms. Water conservation becomes critical. This is when the story opens — if set in the hot season, the political crisis maps onto environmental stress.
- **Monsoon (June–September):** The southwest monsoon brings heavy but erratic rainfall. Black cotton soil becomes a treacherous swelling clay — impassable for heavy carts, cracking into deep fissures when it dries. Military campaigns traditionally halt.
- **Post-monsoon (October–February):** The comfortable season. Harvest. Festival season. Political assemblies and diplomatic receptions.

**For the story's timeline (814–815 CE):** If Govinda III dies in mid-814, the succession crisis would unfold through the hot season and monsoon — a period when travel is difficult, information moves slowly, and political conspiracies have time to germinate in isolation.

## 2. The Succession Crisis

### Govinda III (r. c. 793–814 CE)

Govinda III was arguably the most powerful Rashtrakuta emperor. His inscriptions — and more importantly, his enemies' inscriptions — confirm an extraordinary military career:

- Defeated the Pratihara emperor Nagabhata II and drove him from Kannauj
- Subdued the Pala king Dharmapala in the east
- Received submission from the Gangas, Cholas, Pandyas, Cheras, and Pallavas in the south
- His Sanjan plates (the longest surviving Rashtrakuta inscription) describe his father Dhruva and himself as world-conquerors (*prithvi-vallabha*)

Govinda III was a Shaiva, but his court was pluralistic. He maintained diplomatic relations with the Abbasid Caliphate (Arab traders were active on the Konkan coast, especially at the port of Thana/Sanjan). His death, around 814 CE, removed the single personality holding together an empire built largely on military dominance and personal charisma.

**Source note:** Dating is approximate. Rashtrakuta chronology relies on reconciling inscriptions with overlapping dating systems (Saka era, regnal years). Fleet's *Dynasties of the Kanarese Districts* (1882) and Altekar's *Rashtrakutas and Their Times* (1934, revised 1967) remain standard references. More recent work by B.P. Sinha and others has refined the dating.

### The Historical Succession

Govinda III was succeeded by his son **Amoghavarsha I** (r. c. 814–878 CE), who would become one of the longest-reigning and most culturally significant Indian rulers. But his accession was not smooth:

- Amoghavarsha was young at accession — possibly as young as 13 or 14. **[INFERRED — his exact birth date is unknown, but his 64-year reign and the timing of his mature-period inscriptions suggest a very young accession.]**
- The early years of his reign (c. 814–821 CE) were consumed by revolts from feudatories and kinsmen.
- The most serious revolt was led by his uncle or cousin (the exact relationship is debated), who may have controlled significant military forces in the northern provinces.
- Amoghavarsha was reportedly driven from Manyakheta at least once during these early revolts.

This seven-year period of instability is exactly the historical vacuum our story inhabits. The question of "who really controls Manyakheta" would have been genuinely uncertain during 814–815 CE.

### The Tripartite Struggle (Geopolitical Context)

The Rashtrakuta succession crisis did not occur in isolation. It was embedded in the century-long **tripartite struggle** — the three-way contest for supremacy in northern India between:

1. **Rashtrakutas** (Deccan): Controlling the middle ground, projecting power northward when strong, retreating to consolidate the Deccan when weak.
2. **Pratiharas** (Rajasthan/western UP): Gurjara-Pratiharas based at Kannauj after Nagabhata II's recovery. Govinda III had humiliated them; they would probe southward at any sign of Rashtrakuta weakness.
3. **Palas** (Bengal/Bihar): The Buddhist Pala dynasty under Dharmapala and later Devapala. Controlled the eastern Gangetic plain and competed with the Pratiharas for Kannauj.

**Why this matters for the plot:** A succession crisis in Manyakheta is not merely a domestic affair. Pratihara spies, Pala agents, and vassals along the northern frontier would all be watching. A forged copper-plate grant — changing a beneficiary — could redirect revenue, military obligations, or territorial control in ways that benefit an external power. The stakes of our murder mystery are imperial, not merely local.

### Plausible Factions in 814 CE

**[INFERRED from inscriptional evidence and political logic]**

The following factions would plausibly compete during the succession:

- **The legitimate heir's party:** Those backing young Amoghavarsha. This would include his mother's family (possibly Ganga connections), loyalist generals, and administrators who owed their positions to Govinda III's patronage system.
- **Rival claimants within the Rashtrakuta family:** Rashtrakuta succession was not strictly primogeniture. Brothers, uncles, and cousins with military followings could press claims. The revolt that historically drove Amoghavarsha from Manyakheta was led by a kinsman.
- **Feudatory kings:** The *mahasamantas* and *samantadhipatis* — vassal rulers who owed allegiance to the Rashtrakuta overlord but commanded their own armies. A weak centre meant renegotiating terms.
- **The Jain establishment:** Amoghavarsha would become a devout Jain, possibly converting formally. The Jain community — wealthy merchants and influential monks — had a strong interest in which ruler took the throne. Their financial power (see Section 6) made them kingmakers.
- **The Brahminical establishment:** The *rajapurohita* and the network of *agrahara* Brahmins whose land grants depended on royal authority. A change in dynasty or a ruler hostile to Brahminical institutions could revoke their grants.
- **Military commanders (*dandanayaka*, *senapati*):** Professional soldiers who controlled fortresses and frontier garrisons. Their loyalty was to whoever could pay and promote them.

## 3. The Rashtrakuta Court

### Administrative Structure

The Rashtrakuta administrative system was sophisticated, drawing on earlier Chalukyan models. Key officials attested in inscriptions:

| Title | Role | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Rajadhiraja / Prithvi-vallabha** | Emperor (supreme sovereign) | The *maharajadhiraja* title appears in formal inscriptions |
| **Yuvaraja** | Crown prince / heir designate | Not always formally appointed |
| **Rajapurohita** | Royal priest | Performs consecration rituals, advises on auspicious timing. Senior Brahmin with direct access to the sovereign |
| **Mahasandhi-vigrahika** | Minister of war and peace (foreign affairs) | Attested in multiple inscriptions. A powerful political role |
| **Mahasamanta / Samantadhipati** | Feudatory king / provincial vassal | Semi-independent rulers owing military service |
| **Dandanayaka** | Military commander | Could command provincial garrisons |

### Copper-Plate Land Grants (*Tamra-sasana*)

This is central to the plot. A forged copper-plate charter is the key evidence. Here is how these documents actually worked:

**Physical description:**
- Two to five (sometimes more) rectangular copper plates, roughly 20–30 cm wide and 15–20 cm tall, and 1–3 mm thick.
- Each plate is inscribed on one or both sides using an iron stylus (*lekhani*). The script is typically an early form of Kannada-Telugu script for Rashtrakuta grants, with Sanskrit for the formal portions and Kannada for boundary descriptions and witness lists.
- Plates are held together by a copper ring threaded through a hole in each plate. The ring is sealed with a copper seal (*mudra*) bearing the royal insignia — for the Rashtrakutas, this was typically the Garuda (eagle) emblem or the boar (*varaha*).
- The seal is soldered or cast shut around the ring, making it theoretically tamper-evident. To remove or replace a plate, you would need to break and recast the seal.

**Contents of a typical grant:**
1. **Invocation (*mangala*):** An auspicious verse, usually to Shiva (for most Rashtrakuta grants) or to Jina/Vishnu depending on the ruler's personal devotion.
2. **Genealogy (*vamsa-varnana*):** A formal recitation of the ruling dynasty's lineage, often going back several generations with florid praise.
3. **Description of the grantor:** The ruling king, with his titles and epithets.
4. **The grant itself:** Identification of the land being granted — village name, district (*vishaya*), boundaries (using landmarks, rivers, trees, neighbouring villages). The beneficiary's name, *gotra* (lineage), Vedic affiliation.
5. **Purpose:** Often "for the merit of my parents and myself" or for specific ritual obligations.
6. **Imprecation (*shapa*):** Curses on anyone who revokes the grant. These are remarkably standard and severe: "He who revokes this grant will be guilty of the five great sins and will dwell in hell for sixty thousand years."
7. **Witness list and date.**
8. **Engraver's colophon:** Sometimes the engraver (*sutradhara* or *tambrakara*) signs his work.

**How could one be forged?**

This is where our plot becomes materially plausible:

- **Plate substitution:** The most feasible method. Remove the seal ring, extract one plate, substitute a new one with altered text, recast the seal. This requires access to a skilled coppersmith and someone who can replicate the engraving style. The seal recasting is the weak point — a careful examiner could detect differences in the copper alloy, casting bubbles, or slight misalignment of the seal impression.

- **Surface erasure and re-engraving:** Copper can be hammered smooth and re-inscribed, but this leaves traces — the surface texture differs, and ghosting of the original text may be visible under oblique light. **This is how Vishvaksena would detect the forgery** — he reads the plates like a textual critic reads a manuscript, looking for interpolations.

- **Entirely fabricated set:** Creating a complete forged grant from scratch. More ambitious but leaves fewer physical traces of alteration. However, it requires knowledge of the correct formulary, genealogical claims, scribal conventions, and must match the archive copy (if one exists at the *akshapatala*).

- **The archive problem:** The *akshapataladhikari*'s office should maintain a palm-leaf or cloth register of grants issued. A forger must either also alter this register or rely on the chaos of a succession crisis (when archives may be unsecured or their keepers compromised) to avoid cross-checking.

**Source note:** The metallurgy and epigraphy of copper-plate grants are well studied. D.C. Sircar's *Indian Epigraphy* (1965) and *Indian Epigraphical Glossary* (1966) are standard references. Actual forged copper plates have been identified by epigraphists — the detection methods described above are historically grounded. The Kanheri plates and certain "Ghatotkacha" grants have been identified as later fabrications through exactly these kinds of material and textual analysis.

### The Role of the *Rajapurohita*

Vishvaksena holds the title *rajapurohita-adhikarin* — the royal priest with administrative authority. This is a historically attested combination. The *rajapurohita* was:

- **Ritual authority:** Performed the *rajyabhisheka* (royal consecration), daily fire rituals for the kingdom's welfare, and auspicious-timing consultations for military campaigns, building projects, and diplomatic initiatives. Without the *rajapurohita*'s ritual approval, a new king's legitimacy was weakened.

- **Political counsellor:** The *Arthashastra* tradition (which was very much alive as a reference text in this period) places the *purohita* among the king's closest advisors. The priest's access to the sovereign was daily and intimate — he performed morning rituals in the royal presence.

- **Textual expert:** A *rajapurohita* would be trained in Vedic literature, *dharmashastra* (legal texts), and *arthashastra* (statecraft). He would be literate in Sanskrit, capable of reading inscriptions, and familiar with the formulary of official documents. This is why Vishvaksena can "read" the crime scene like a disputed land grant — documentary criticism is literally part of his professional training.

- **Judicial role:** Brahmins served as assessors in disputes, particularly those involving *dharma* (religious law), inheritance, and property. The *rajapurohita* could be called to adjudicate disputes over grants, ritual precedence, and caste matters.

**A 52-year-old Smarta Brahmin *rajapurohita* in 814 CE** would be at the peak of his authority. "Smarta" means he follows the *smriti* tradition — orthodox but ecumenical within Hinduism, accepting multiple deities (unlike a strictly Shaiva or Vaishnava sectarian). This positions Vishvaksena as someone who can navigate between different religious factions at court — he is Brahminical establishment but not aligned with any single temple faction.

### Religious Pluralism at Court

The Rashtrakutas are remarkable for their ecumenical patronage. This was not mere tolerance — it was political strategy:

- **Shaiva institutions:** Govinda III's personal devotion was Shaiva, and the Kailasa temple at Ellora (commissioned by his grandfather Krishna I, c. 760 CE) is a Shaiva monument. Shaiva mathas (monasteries) received royal patronage and their heads (*mahants*) had political influence.

- **Vaishnava institutions:** Also patronized. Rashtrakuta inscriptions sometimes invoke both Shiva and Vishnu. Vaishnava temples at Ellora (e.g., the Dashavatara cave) date from this period.

- **Jain institutions:** This is the most politically significant. The Rashtrakutas had deep Jain connections from their earliest period. Amoghavarsha I (the incoming ruler) would become a devout Jain, authoring the Kannada text *Kavirajamarga* and patronizing Jain scholars like Jinasena. Jain monks (*munis*) served as royal tutors and advisors. Jain merchants financed military campaigns and received commercial privileges in return.

- **Buddhist presence:** Diminishing by this period in the western Deccan but not extinct. The caves at Ajanta (abandoned by now) and Ellora (still active) attest to Buddhist presence. Buddhist *viharas* may have survived in Manyakheta as educational centres.

**The competition:** Religious patronage was zero-sum in practice. A land grant to a Jain basadi was a grant that did not go to a Shaiva matha. The succession of a Jain-sympathetic ruler (Amoghavarsha) versus a Shaiva loyalist would directly affect the flow of royal patronage — temples, *agraharas*, tax exemptions, and commercial privileges. This is a plausible motive for murder.

## 4. Material Culture for Visual Grounding

### Clothing

**Source note:** Almost all clothing descriptions are derived from sculptural evidence at Ellora (c. 760–850 CE), Pattadakal (c. 740 CE), and Badami (c. 550–750 CE, earlier but stylistically continuous). Textile evidence does not survive; we reconstruct from carved figures and occasional literary descriptions.

**Vishvaksena (senior Brahmin, *rajapurohita*):**
- **Lower garment:** White unstitched cotton *dhoti* (*vasana*), wrapped in the *panchakaccha* style (drawn between the legs and tucked at the back, creating a trouser-like effect for ease of movement while maintaining modesty). Knee-length or slightly below. White is the Brahminical marker — absolutely no indigo, lac-dyed red, or other colours for a ritualist of this orthodoxy.
- **Upper body:** The sacred thread (*yajnopavita*) across the left shoulder is the single most important visual marker — three twisted cotton cords (or multiples of three for a senior ritualist who has performed many sacrifices). Upper body may be bare in warm weather, or covered with a white cotton *uttariya* (shawl/wrap) draped over one shoulder. In formal court settings, the *uttariya* would be arranged precisely.
- **Hair:** Shaved head with a *shikha* (tuft/topknot) at the crown. At 52, possibly greying. No facial hair or a closely trimmed beard (both are attested for Brahmins in this period; sculptural evidence at Ellora shows both shaved and bearded priestly figures).
- **Forehead marks:** *Tripundra* (three horizontal lines of sacred ash/*vibhuti*) if Shaiva-leaning, or *urdhvapundra* (vertical sandalwood paste mark) if Vaishnava-leaning. As a Smarta, Vishvaksena likely wears vibhuti — the ecumenical option.
- **Accessories:** Rudraksha-seed necklace or mala (prayer beads). Possibly earrings (small gold studs — Ellora sculptures show Brahmin figures with earrings). Simple wooden or leather sandals (*paduka*), removed before entering any sacred space.
- **No weapons.** A Brahmin of this orthodoxy would not carry arms. His authority is intellectual and ritual.

**Governor Rajamalla (provincial administrator, likely Kshatriya):**
- **Lower garment:** *Dhoti* in finer cotton or silk, possibly with a coloured border (indigo, turmeric yellow). Longer and more voluminous than a Brahmin's functional wrap.
- **Upper body:** A short *kanchuka* (stitched jacket/tunic) for formal occasions — this is attested in Rashtrakuta-period sculpture. Over this, a fine *uttariya*. In death, whatever he was wearing at the time of his murder: possibly sleeping garments (simpler *dhoti*, bare upper body) if killed at night, or administrative dress if during the day.
- **Jewellery:** Gold necklaces, armlets (*keyura*), and earrings. A signet ring (*mudrika*) would be essential for a governor — used to authenticate documents. This ring may be missing from the body (evidence of theft or evidence removal).
- **Hair:** Likely a turban (*ushnisha*) for formal occasions. Hair worn long, gathered up, or in a topknot for Kshatriya males.
- **The body itself:** Rajamalla as a governor would be well-fed. Rashtrakuta-period sculpture depicts men of status with solid builds.

**A Jain merchant:**
- **Lower garment:** White *dhoti*, fine cotton or silk. Jain laity favoured white as a purity marker (paralleling Brahminical practice but for different doctrinal reasons — *ahimsa*, non-violence, extends to not using dyed cloth where insects may have been killed in the dyeing process).
- **Upper body:** *Uttariya* or light *kanchuka*. Possibly minimal — Jain laity in the Digambara tradition (predominant in the Deccan) tended toward simplicity, though wealthy merchants were not ascetics.
- **Jewellery:** Less than a Kshatriya governor but not absent. Gold earrings, a simple necklace. The visual marker would be prosperity combined with restraint.
- **Distinctive:** A Jain merchant might carry a *rajoharana* (small broom of soft threads) or a mouth-cloth — though these are more strictly monastic items, devout laity sometimes carried them.

**A *kayastha* scribe:**
- **Lower garment:** Cotton *dhoti*, clean but not luxurious. Possibly ink-stained at the hem.
- **Upper body:** Bare or simple *uttariya*. The visual markers are professional: ink-stained fingers, a writing reed (*lekhani*) tucked behind the ear or into the waistband, a cloth satchel for palm-leaf documents.
- **Hair:** Similar to Brahmin style (topknot) but without the sacred thread. This absence is the key visual caste marker.
- **Posture and body language:** A scribe sits cross-legged with the writing surface on his lap or on a low wooden stand. Years of this posture produce a characteristic forward lean.

### Architecture and Interiors

**Palace audience hall (*sabha-mandapa*):**
- Pillared hall with columns in the Rashtrakuta style: lathe-turned columns with cushion capitals (visible at Ellora, Pattadakal). Columns of polished dark basalt or grey granite.
- Floor: Polished stone slabs, possibly with inlaid geometric patterns. Swept and washed daily.
- Seating: Low stone benches along the walls for courtiers. The throne (*simhasana*) would be a raised stone or wood seat with carved lion armrests (following the literal meaning: "lion-seat"). Cushions of cotton or silk.
- Lighting: Open to air on two or more sides (pillared verandahs). For evening audiences, oil lamps: brass or bronze *deepa* stands holding multiple cotton wicks in sesame or coconut oil. The light would be warm and flickering, with pools of shadow between columns.
- Ceiling: Carved stone (if cut from rock, as at Ellora) or wooden beams with carved panels. Possibly painted — Rashtrakuta painting survives at Ellora (Kailasa temple painted ceilings) in red, ochre, black, and white.

**Jain basadi (temple):**
- Smaller and more austere than the major Hindu temples. Dravida-style but less ornate externally.
- Interior: A simple pillared *mandapa* leading to a *garbhagriha* (sanctum) housing a Jina image — typically a seated or standing *tirthankara* figure in polished stone, often dark schist or granite.
- The floor would be polished stone, kept immaculately clean (Jain ritual purity standards are strict).
- Minimal furniture: no fire altar (Jains do not perform fire rituals). Oil lamps and flower offerings at the image. Possibly a reading stand for scriptural recitation.
- **For the crime scene:** The area near the basadi threshold would be a transitional space — covered portico or *mandapa*, open to the courtyard. This is where the body lies. The visual grammar: austere stone, careful geometry, and then the violent intrusion of a dead body.

**Administrative office (*akshapatala*):**
- A secure room or set of rooms within the palace complex.
- Storage: Copper plates wrapped in cloth and stored in wooden chests or stone niches. Palm-leaf manuscripts (*tada-patra*) bundled between wooden boards and tied with cord. These are the archives.
- Work surfaces: Low wooden desks (*phalaka*) for scribes, with ink pots (carbon-black ink for palm leaf, iron stylus work for copper), pen-cases, and sand for blotting.
- The smell: Old palm leaf (slightly astringent, vegetal), lamp oil, copper.

### Objects

**Copper plates and engraving tools:**
- The plates themselves: hand-hammered copper sheets, irregular edges, slightly uneven surface. The engraving is done with an iron stylus pressed and dragged to cut the characters — not hammered or punched. The resulting script is angular and somewhat rough compared to written manuscripts.
- A scribe working copper needs: a flat stone or wooden surface to support the plate, the iron stylus, a magnifying device (rock crystal lenses are attested in ancient India, though **[SPECULATIVE]** for this specific context), and good light.

**Seal rings (*mudrika*, *anguliyaka*):**
- Cut from cornelian, agate, or cast in gold or bronze.
- Incised with the owner's name, title, or emblem in mirror-reverse so it prints correctly in sealing wax or clay.
- A governor's seal ring would be an object of power — losing it or having it stolen compromises documentary security.

**Brahminical ritual paraphernalia:**
- Fire altar (*agni-kunda*): A square or circular pit of fired brick, with precisely measured dimensions. For the *rajapurohita*'s daily ritual, a small portable hearth.
- Offerings: Ghee (clarified butter), sesame seeds, rice, sandalwood sticks, dried herbs. Contained in small bronze or copper vessels (*patra*).
- The *kamandalu*: A water vessel, typically made of gourd or metal, carried by Brahmin ascetics and ritualists. Vishvaksena would have one.
- *Darbha* grass: Sacred grass used in all Vedic rituals, gathered in a specific way.

### Food and Social Customs

**Vishvaksena's diet:**
A strict Smarta Brahmin in this period would eat only food prepared by a Brahmin or by himself. Vegetarian, excluding onions and garlic (classified as *tamasic*, stimulating, in the *dharmashastra* dietary codes). His daily meals:
- Morning: rice gruel or *payasa* (milk-rice) after completing morning rituals.
- Midday meal: Boiled rice, *dal* (lentil preparation), vegetable dishes (gourd, beans, leafy greens), ghee, buttermilk. Eaten seated on the floor on a banana leaf or bronze plate. Eating with the right hand only.
- Evening: Light — milk, fruits, possibly leftover rice.
- Important: He would not eat at another's house without knowing the cook's caste status. At court, separate Brahmin kitchens were maintained for this purpose.

**Court feasting:**
A Rashtrakuta royal feast would be lavish. Arab travellers describe Indian courts as serving hundreds of dishes. For non-Brahmin courtiers: meat dishes (goat, venison, fowl, fish), along with rice, breads, multiple vegetable and lentil preparations, sweets, and fruit. Wine (*sura*, *madhu*) from grapes, rice, or palm sap. Musicians and dancers would accompany formal banquets.

**Betel-chewing (*tambula*):**
Ubiquitous as a social ritual. Betel leaf wrapped around areca nut, lime paste, and sometimes cardamom or clove. Offered after meals and during any formal meeting as a gesture of hospitality and closure. Refusing betel is a minor social slight. A betel-stained mouth (red-brown) is a standard visual detail for any character who is not an ascetic. The betel set — a brass or silver *tambula-patra* — is a status object.

## 5. Caste and Pollution in 9th-Century Deccan

### Purity and Pollution Rules in Practice

**What Vishvaksena cannot do:**

The *dharmashastra* literature (Manu, Yajnavalkya, and their commentaries, which were the operative legal-ritual texts) prescribes specific pollution rules:

- **Contact with a corpse (*mrtaka-ashaucha*):** Touching a dead body creates ritual impurity lasting 10 days for family members. For a non-family Brahmin, simply entering the space where death occurred creates a lesser but real pollution that requires purificatory bathing and ritual. **This is why Vishvaksena examines the scene from the threshold.** He cannot enter the space where Rajamalla lies dead without incurring *ashaucha* that would disqualify him from performing rituals — including the royal rituals that are his primary duty and source of authority.

- **Entering spaces associated with death, butchery, or leather-working.** A *chandala*'s quarter is permanently polluted in this worldview.

- **Accepting food or water from lower castes.** Accepting water from a *chandala* would require extensive purification.

- **Physical contact with menstruating women, leather goods made by lower castes, certain animals.**

**How rigid were these boundaries in the Rashtrakuta period?**

This is an important nuance. The Rashtrakutas were more pluralistic than later Deccan dynasties:

- The Rashtrakutas themselves were likely of mixed or contested caste origin. Their genealogical claims to Kshatriya status (and specifically to descent from the Yadava lineage) are characteristic of upwardly mobile dynasties who needed Brahminical legitimation. This means the relationship between the Rashtrakuta rulers and the Brahminical establishment was mutually dependent, not one of simple dominance.
- Jain influence moderated strict caste hierarchy in commercial and some political contexts. Jain doctrine rejects the *varna* system's ritual basis (though Jain communities in practice maintained social stratification).
- Inscriptional evidence shows that individuals of non-elite caste backgrounds could achieve high administrative positions. *Kayastha* scribes, for instance, wielded real power despite their ambiguous ritual status.
- **However:** Brahminical pollution rules for Brahmins themselves were internally enforced. A *rajapurohita* who violated pollution norms would lose credibility among his own community and with the orthodox faction at court. Vishvaksena's refusal to enter the polluted space is not mere squeamishness — it is professional survival.

**[IMPORTANT NUANCE for the story:]** The gap between doctrinal rule and social reality is productive for fiction. Vishvaksena *must* avoid the corpse to maintain his authority, but this same restriction means he cannot directly examine the evidence. He is the most qualified investigator but the most physically constrained. This is the dramatic engine of the episode.

### The *Chandala* Settlement

**Location:** Outside the city walls, downwind (typically to the south or southwest, given prevailing wind patterns). Separated from the main settlement by a clear boundary — a stream, a ditch, or simply open ground. *Dharmashastric* texts prescribe that *chandalas* must announce their presence when approaching a settlement (by striking a wooden clapper or calling out) so that upper-caste residents can avoid contact.

**Actual occupations (not just "untouchable"):**
- **Leather-working (*charmakara*):** Processing animal hides, making leather goods. This requires knowledge of tanning agents — tree bark, mineral salts.
- **Disposal of dead:** Carrying corpses to the cremation ground, maintaining the cremation pyres, handling the remains. This gives them intimate familiarity with death and its physical signs.
- **Executioners and punishers:** Some *dharmashastra* texts assign judicial punishment (carrying out sentences) to the *chandala* community.
- **Night-soil removal and sanitation.**
- **Relevant to the plot — medicinal/poison knowledge:** Communities engaged in butchery, leather-working, and corpse-handling developed extensive practical knowledge of animal and plant chemistry. They knew which plants and substances accelerated decomposition, which preserved, which killed. This knowledge was empirical and hereditary, transmitted within the community. A *chandala* elder would know more about the physical signs of poisoning than any Brahmin physician trained in *Ayurveda* texts — because they had direct observational experience with death in all its forms. **This is how the plot can plausibly bring Vishvaksena into contact with *chandala* knowledge while maintaining the social distance he is compelled to observe.**

**Source note:** The social reality of the *chandala* and related communities is documented in *dharmashastra* texts (prescriptive, not descriptive) and, more usefully, in inscriptions recording grants to or regulations of such communities. The Arthashastra of Kautilya describes their prescribed settlements. However, almost all sources are written by upper-caste authors — the *chandala* community's own perspective is unrecoverable from this period.

### The *Kayastha* (Scribe Caste)

The *kayastha* community occupied a crucial and contested social position:

- **Function:** Professional scribes, accountants, record-keepers. They were the civil service — the people who actually ran the administration below the level of appointed ministers.
- **Caste status:** Ambiguous and disputed. Brahminical texts variously classify *kayasthas* as a mixed caste (*varna-sankara*), sometimes disparagingly. Some *kayastha* communities claimed Kshatriya or even Brahmin origin. The reality: they were a literate, powerful, administratively indispensable community whose ritual status did not match their actual influence.
- **Relationship to Brahmins:** Tense. Brahmins and *kayasthas* competed for administrative positions. Brahmins had ritual superiority; *kayasthas* had practical indispensability. A *rajapurohita* would work alongside *kayasthas* daily but would not dine with them.
- **Relationship to power:** *Kayasthas* controlled the documentary machinery of the state. They drafted the charters, maintained the archives, kept the revenue records. A *kayastha* who wanted to forge a copper-plate grant would have the technical knowledge and the access.

**For the plot:** The *kayastha* scribe is the obvious suspect — he has means and opportunity. But that obvious suspicion is itself a tool of misdirection, because everyone at court knows the *kayasthas* have documentary access.

## 6. The Jain Community in Manyakheta

### Jain Merchants and Their Power

By the 9th century, the Jain community in the Deccan was extraordinarily wealthy and politically connected:

- **Trade networks:** Jain merchants controlled significant portions of the long-distance trade between the western coast (Konkan ports: Sanjan/Thana, Goa, Honavar) and the interior Deccan. Key commodities: cotton textiles (the Deccan's major export), horses (imported from Arabia via the coast), spices, precious stones, and metalware.
- **Banking and credit:** Jain merchant families operated as bankers (*setthi*, *shreshthi*), extending credit to the state, to feudatories, and to other merchants. This financial power translated directly into political influence — a king who needed to fund a military campaign might borrow from Jain *setthis* and repay with land grants and commercial privileges.
- **Guild organization (*sreni*):** Merchants were organized into guilds by trade speciality. These guilds had their own internal governance, dispute resolution, and could negotiate collectively with the state. The guild head (*sreni-pramukha*) was a major civic figure.

**Source note:** Jain merchant power in the Deccan is well attested in inscriptions from this period. The *Epigraphia Carnatica* volumes contain numerous records of Jain merchants endowing temples, funding irrigation works, and receiving royal privileges. The *Aihole inscription* (of earlier Chalukya period, but the pattern continues) records guild structures.

### Jain Basadis in Manyakheta

- **Architecture:** Jain temples in the Deccan follow the Dravida style but with doctrinal modifications. No processional *vimana* (tower over the sanctum) in some traditions; others do build towers. Typically more restrained decoration than Hindu temples — fewer narrative friezes, more geometric abstraction, and prominent placement of *tirthankara* images.
- **Daily life at a basadi:** Monks perform *puja* (though Jain puja is non-theistic — veneration of the *tirthankara* as a model, not a god). Lay devotees visit for morning and evening worship. The basadi also functions as a community centre and school. Jain scriptural recitation (*svadhyaya*) would be audible from within.
- **The basadi in the crime scene:** A body found near a Jain basadi is symbolically loaded. It pollutes a sacred space. For the Jain community, this is an act of aggression — whether intentional or not. The Jain monks would want the body removed immediately and the space ritually purified. This creates time pressure on the investigation.

### The Merchant Council (*Panchayat* / *Sreni-sabha*)

- **Composition:** Elders of the major merchant families, typically five (*pancha*) senior members, hence *panchayat*. In practice, the council could be larger.
- **Jurisdiction:** Internal community disputes (contract disagreements, property, marriage). But in practice, powerful merchant councils also adjudicated matters involving non-Jain parties if commercial interests were involved. They could impose fines, expulsion from the guild (economic death), and social boycott.
- **Relationship to royal authority:** Semi-autonomous. The king recognized guild jurisdiction over commercial matters in exchange for tax revenue and political loyalty. A shrewd merchant council could delay or withhold tax payments as political leverage.
- **For the plot:** The Jain merchant council has its own interests in the succession — specifically, they want a ruler sympathetic to Jain institutions. They also have the financial resources to commission a forgery and the network to execute it. They are not necessarily the villains, but they are players.

### Amoghavarsha I's Jain Connection

Amoghavarsha I (who is about to take the throne as our story begins) would become:
- A formal Jain patron, possibly a convert (the *Kavirajamarga* tradition attributes personal Jain devotion to him)
- The patron of Jinasena, who composed the *Adipurana* (a major Jain cosmological text in Sanskrit)
- A king who reportedly offered his own finger to the goddess Mahalakshmi during a crisis (mixing Hindu and Jain devotion in ways that annoyed purists on both sides)

**This means:** The Jain community in 814 CE knows that the incoming young ruler is sympathetic to them. They have every reason to support Amoghavarsha's smooth succession — and every reason to fear that a rival claimant might reverse their privileged position. If Rajamalla's altered charter redirects resources away from Jain beneficiaries (or toward them, as a false flag), the Jain community's involvement becomes a key investigative question.

## 7. Uncertainty Flags

This section documents what we know, what we are inferring, and what is honestly speculative. This is critical for maintaining intellectual honesty in the production.

### What we know (attested in inscriptions, archaeology, or contemporary foreign accounts):

- The Rashtrakuta dynasty, its general chronology, and the succession from Govinda III to Amoghavarsha I
- The existence and approximate location of Manyakheta (modern Malkhed)
- Rashtrakuta architectural style (from Ellora, Pattadakal, Badami)
- The tripartite struggle between Rashtrakutas, Pratiharas, and Palas
- The format and contents of copper-plate land grants (hundreds survive)
- Administrative titles (from inscriptions)
- Rashtrakuta religious pluralism (attested in patronage inscriptions)
- Jain merchant power in the Deccan (inscriptional evidence)
- Amoghavarsha I's Jain connections (from his own inscriptions and literary works)
- General caste-occupation structure (from *dharmashastra* and inscriptions, noting both are elite-authored sources)
- Arab traders' accounts of Rashtrakuta wealth and status (Al-Masudi, Sulaiman)

### What we are inferring (reasonable extrapolation from evidence):

- **[INFERRED]** Manyakheta's physical layout — based on analogy with other Deccan capitals and the general patterns of early medieval Indian urbanism. No archaeological plan of Rashtrakuta-era Manyakheta exists.
- **[INFERRED]** Specific clothing details — from sculptural evidence at sites 50–100 years older or later, assuming stylistic continuity. Actual textiles do not survive.
- **[INFERRED]** Sensory details (sounds, smells) — from literary descriptions across several centuries of Indian texts, not specifically from 814 CE Manyakheta.
- **[INFERRED]** The specific administrative titles in use at Manyakheta in 814 CE — compiled from inscriptions spanning the entire Rashtrakuta period (753–973 CE). Not all titles may have been current simultaneously.
- **[INFERRED]** Amoghavarsha I's age at accession — from the length of his reign and the approximate dates of his mature-period activities.
- **[INFERRED]** *Chandala* community knowledge of poisons and toxicology — from their documented occupational roles and reasonable inference about empirical knowledge. No direct textual source describes *chandala* toxicological expertise.

### What is honestly speculative (our fiction fills these gaps):

- **[SPECULATIVE]** The specific layout of buildings, streets, and quarters within Manyakheta. We are building a plausible city, not reconstructing an actual one.
- **[SPECULATIVE]** The existence of a governor named Rajamalla and a *rajapurohita* named Vishvaksena. These are fictional characters with historically accurate roles.
- **[SPECULATIVE]** The specific mechanism of charter forgery in this case. We know forgery was possible and was detected in the historical record; the specific plot device is invented.
- **[SPECULATIVE]** The degree of Jain political organization in Manyakheta in 814 CE specifically. Jain political influence is well attested in the Deccan generally, but we are imagining its specific institutional form at this moment and place.
- **[SPECULATIVE]** Any dialogue, interpersonal dynamics, or specific events. All characters and plot events are fictional.

### Honest gaps our fiction exploits:

1. **The early years of Amoghavarsha I's reign (814–821 CE) are poorly documented.** We have inscriptions from before and after this period, but the succession crisis itself is known mainly from later references to the revolts Amoghavarsha faced. This documentary silence is our creative space.

2. **Manyakheta's physical destruction means we cannot be "wrong" about specific buildings.** Unlike writing about, say, Vijayanagara (where substantial ruins survive and constrain fiction), Manyakheta gives us freedom to build a plausible city.

3. **Internal court politics leave no inscriptional record.** Inscriptions record outcomes (grants, victories, genealogies), not the conspiracies, murders, and power struggles that produced them. Our entire murder-mystery plot inhabits this silence.

4. **Lower-caste perspectives are absent from the record.** The *chandala* characters in our story cannot be based on their own testimony. We must write them with awareness that we are imagining lives that the historical record systematically excludes.
