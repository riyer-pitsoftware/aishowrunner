## Scene 1 — The Copper Plate (Visual Jolt)

**Timestamp:** 0:00–0:05
**Duration:** 5s
**Beat:** Frame 1 — Visual Jolt

**Visual description:**
Extreme close-up of a copper plate filling 80% of the frame. Angled oblique light from the upper left — a single oil lamp just out of frame — catches the engraved Kannada script, casting micro-shadows in the chisel grooves. The copper surface is dark with patina, Temple Stone (#B8956A) and Chola Bronze (#8B5E3C) dominating. Foreground: the tips of two fingers holding the plate's edge, skin weathered, sacred thread (yajnopavita) just visible crossing the wrist. Middle ground: the plate itself, text legible but foreign. Background: soft darkness, a faint warm glow suggesting a room beyond. Slow push-in over 5 seconds. A scratch across one line of text — visible even in the close-up — where something has been erased and re-engraved.

**Audio:**
No narration. Sound only: the faint creak of a copper plate being tilted. A distant temple bell, single strike, decaying.

**Purpose:** Stop the scroll. Material intrigue — the viewer sees evidence of tampering before they understand what it means. The scratch is the mystery in miniature.

**Prompt notes:** Photorealistic copper texture is essential. The patina must look aged — dark brown-green, not shiny new copper. Oblique lighting must create visible shadows in the engraved grooves. The scratch/erasure must be noticeable but not cartoonish — a slightly different surface texture where text was scraped and re-cut. Fingers should be dark-skinned, aged, with visible sacred thread. No face visible. Depth of field: plate sharp, everything else soft.

Vishvaksena is a silhouette only — no facial detail yet, just the outline of a dhoti-wrapped figure with shaved head. The doorway behind him should glow with hot Monsoon Ivory (#F0E6D2) light. Avoid any modern elements. The pillar base in foreground should have simple geometric carving consistent with early Rashtrakuta style.

## Scene 3 — The Deccan Plateau

**Timestamp:** 0:10–0:30
**Duration:** 20s
**Beat:** Context Drop

**Visual description:**
Wide aerial-perspective shot of the northern Deccan plateau at dawn. Red-brown laterite earth stretching to a hazy horizon. Foreground: a cluster of temple shikhara towers and whitewashed walls — Manyakheta from the south, elevated on rocky ground. Middle ground: the city sprawl, cotton awnings visible as pale specks, a processional road. Background: flat plateau dissolving into warm atmospheric haze, a seasonal river bed (dry, cracked black cotton soil). Lighting: early morning sun from the right, long shadows, warm golden-amber cast. Palette: Temple Stone ground, Monsoon Ivory sky at horizon, Lamp Black shadows.

**Text overlay:** Small, bottom-left, Monsoon Ivory, thin rule above:
`Manyakheta (modern Malkhed, Karnataka) — Capital of the Rashtrakuta Empire`

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "Manyakheta. 814 CE. The Rashtrakutas ruled from this plateau — an empire the Arab geographers counted among four greatest on earth. Their king Govinda the Third had humiliated every rival from Bengal to Rajasthan. Then he died. And left a boy on the throne."

*Sound:* Wind across open land. Distant cattle.

**Purpose:** Geographic and political context in 20 seconds. Establishes scale — this is not a small story. The narration drops three anchors: place, power, vulnerability.

**Prompt notes:** The city should feel substantial but not gleaming — this is weathered stone, not fantasy. Temples in Dravida style (stepped pyramidal towers). The dry riverbed signals the hot season. Atmospheric haze is critical — this is 40°C heat. No green lush vegetation — scrubby neem trees, brown grass. The palette must stay warm: no cool blues anywhere. Sky should be pale warm ivory, not blue.

## Scene 4 — The Boy King

**Timestamp:** 0:30–0:50
**Duration:** 20s
**Beat:** Character Introduction (Part 1 — the political context figure)

**Visual description:**
Interior: the sabha-mandapa (audience hall). Low angle looking up at a stone throne platform. A young figure — Amoghavarsha I, perhaps 14 — sits rigid on a carved stone seat. He is small against the architecture. Foreground: the back of a courtier's head and shoulder, slightly out of focus, establishing the viewer as one of the assembly. Middle ground: the boy-king, lit by a shaft of light from a clerestory opening above, wearing a fine dhoti with gold-thread border, minimal jewelry for his age — armlets, a single necklace. His face is composed but his hands grip the seat arms. Background: massive carved pillars receding into darkness, the suggestion of more figures in shadow. Palette dominated by Lamp Black and Temple Stone, with the shaft of light creating a Monsoon Ivory pool around the throne.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "Amoghavarsha was thirteen. Maybe fourteen. He had inherited an empire and a court full of men who remembered his father's conquests. Men who had their own ambitions. The first to test him would be a dead man."

*Sound:* Low murmur of assembled court — indistinct voices. Fabric rustling.

**Purpose:** Introduce the political stakes. The boy-king is visually isolated, surrounded by shadow and stone — power that dwarfs him. The narration's final sentence pivots to the murder.

**Prompt notes:** Amoghavarsha must look young — slim, uncertain posture despite regal clothing. The throne should be carved stone, not gold — Rashtrakuta aesthetic is monumental stone, not Mughal opulence. The courtier silhouette in foreground creates depth and the feeling of being watched. Pillars should have the heavy proportions of Ellora/Pattadakal. No ornate painted surfaces — raw carved stone. The single light source from above is theatrical but motivated by architecture.

## Scene 5 — Vishvaksena's Commission

**Timestamp:** 0:50–1:15
**Duration:** 25s
**Beat:** The Question

**Visual description:**
Medium shot: Vishvaksena in three-quarter profile, facing right. First clear view of the protagonist. He stands in the audience hall, hands folded at his chest — not in supplication but in formal address. Shaved head with grey shikha (topknot). Tripundra (three horizontal ash lines) on his forehead. Sacred thread across left shoulder over bare chest. White panchakaccha dhoti, unadorned. Rudraksha mala around his neck. No weapons, no jewelry. Kamandalu (brass water vessel) set on the ground beside his right foot. Lit from above-left — deep shadows carve his face. Age shows: lines around the eyes, weathered skin, the stillness of a man who has outlasted several administrations. Background: soft warm blur of the hall, a carved pillar. Foreground: edge of a stone step.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "The boy-king summoned his rajapurohita. Chief priest. Chief administrator. The man who drafted every charter, sealed every land grant, authenticated every royal command. Vishvaksena had served Govinda. Now he would serve the son. His first task was not a prayer."

*Sound:* Silence — the court murmur drops away. A single footstep on stone.

**Text overlay:** Bottom-left, Monsoon Ivory:
`Rajapurohita-adhikarin — Chief priest and senior administrative officer`

**Purpose:** Character introduction through visual detail and role description. The narration defines Vishvaksena by function, not biography. The viewer understands: this man is the institutional memory.

**Prompt notes:** This is the hero portrait. Every material must be right: the sacred thread is cotton, not gold. The dhoti is white cotton, not silk. Rudraksha beads are rough dark-brown seed husks, not polished gems. The tripundra is vibhuti (sacred ash) — pale grey-white, three horizontal lines across the forehead. The kamandalu is brass with dark patina, small and simple. His body type: lean, not muscular — a lifetime of intellectual rather than physical labor. Expression: watchful, not fierce. Eyes are the focal point.

## Scene 6 — The Basadi Approach

**Timestamp:** 1:15–1:45
**Duration:** 30s
**Beat:** First Narrative Movement

**Visual description:**
Wide shot: a Jain basadi (temple) exterior in harsh midday sun. The building is modest — dressed stone walls, a stepped pyramidal tower, carved entrance with a pillared porch. Hard tropical light from directly above creates deep black shadows under the porch overhang and eaves. Three depth planes: foreground, a pair of royal guards flanking the path, spears upright, casting long shadows on red-brown earth; middle ground, Vishvaksena walking alone toward the basadi entrance, his white dhoti almost blinding in the sun; background, the basadi facade, with a dark doorway and the suggestion of carved figures on the walls. Dust in the air. A cotton awning over a nearby stall, sagging in the heat.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "Governor Rajamalla's body had been found at dawn. At the threshold of a Jain basadi. Not inside. Not outside. On the threshold. In this world, that mattered. Where a man died determined who could touch him, who could examine him, who could even look."

*Sound:* Hard bright ambiance — cicadas, distant street sounds. Footsteps on packed earth. Heat.

**Purpose:** Movement toward the crime scene. The narration introduces the procedural constraint — pollution rules — without lecturing. "In this world, that mattered" does the heavy lifting.

**Prompt notes:** The sun should feel oppressive. Deep shadows, bright highlights, minimal midtones — a high-contrast scene. Vishvaksena's white dhoti should be the brightest element, drawing the eye. Guards should be in the Rashtrakuta military style — no Mughal influence. The basadi should be modest but dignified — this is a Jain temple, not a Hindu royal temple. Carved tirthankara figures on the facade if visible. Red laterite earth, grey-black basalt blocks. No lush vegetation — dry, dusty, hot-season Deccan.

## Scene 7 — The Threshold

**Timestamp:** 1:45–2:00
**Duration:** 15s
**Beat:** Micro-hook

**Visual description:**
Close-medium shot from inside the basadi porch, looking out. Vishvaksena stands at the threshold — his feet precisely at the carved stone sill, not crossing. He looks down. We see what he sees: at the bottom of the frame, a pair of feet. The dead governor's feet, decorated with gold ankle rings, emerging from the bottom edge. The body is barely visible — just the feet and the edge of a fine dhoti hem with a colored border. Between Vishvaksena's feet and the body: the carved threshold stone, worn smooth by centuries of passage. Lighting: bright sun behind Vishvaksena, interior shadow ahead, the body in the transitional light of the threshold. Vishvaksena's face is partially shadowed.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "He stopped at the threshold. A corpse made ground impure. A Brahmin who crossed that line could not perform his duties until he had purified himself. So Vishvaksena did what he always did. He read."

*Sound:* Silence. A fly buzzing — close, intimate.

**Purpose:** Micro-hook. "He read." — the pivot from religious constraint to investigative method. This is the core metaphor of the episode: Vishvaksena reads crime scenes the way he reads documents.

**Prompt notes:** The framing is crucial — we must see Vishvaksena's bare feet at the threshold and the dead man's feet beyond, separated by the carved sill. This is the visual representation of the pollution boundary. The gold ankle rings on the corpse establish wealth. The fine dhoti establishes status. Do NOT show the body's face or wounds — only the feet. The threshold stone should have visible wear. Interior darkness should suggest the basadi's carved interior without showing it clearly.

## Scene 8 — Reading the Body

**Timestamp:** 2:00–2:45
**Duration:** 45s
**Beat:** Rising Action (Part 1)

**Visual description:**
Low angle from near floor level, looking up at Vishvaksena from below the threshold, as if from the corpse's perspective. He stands above, framed by the basadi doorway, silhouetted against bright sky. His expression is intense, focused — studying something below the camera. Warm chiaroscuro: oil lamp placed at the threshold's edge (he has brought light to the shadow). The lamp flame is visible in the lower left, creating an amber glow that catches the carved doorframe and Vishvaksena's face from below. Three planes: foreground lamp and carved doorframe edge, middle Vishvaksena's figure, background bright sky and the basadi tower.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "Rajamalla lay on his back. Well-dressed. Gold at his neck, his arms. The kind of man whose body announced his rank before he opened his mouth. But two things were wrong. Sindoor on his forehead. A Shaiva mark. And vibhuti ash on his chest. A mark used in Jain death rites. Someone had dressed this corpse for two different funerals."

*Sound:* Ambient drone building slowly. The fly again. Oil lamp crackling faintly.

**Purpose:** The first investigative beat. The mixed funerary marks are the first clue — they signal deliberate confusion. The narration delivers forensic detail in short, punchy sentences.

**Prompt notes:** The low angle makes Vishvaksena imposing — we see him as the dead man would. The oil lamp must be a simple clay diya with sesame oil and cotton wick, not a modern lamp. The carved doorframe should show Jain motifs — possibly a tirthankara in a small niche. Do NOT show the body itself — the camera is at body level but pointed UP. The bright sky behind Vishvaksena should be Monsoon Ivory, not blue. His expression: analytical, not horrified — this is a man reading evidence.

## Scene 9 — The Missing Ring

**Timestamp:** 2:45–3:30
**Duration:** 45s
**Beat:** Rising Action (Part 2)

**Visual description:**
Close-up: Rajamalla's hand, palm up, lying on the stone threshold. The hand is adorned — gold armlet visible on the wrist, rings on three fingers. But the index finger of the right hand has a pale band of untanned skin where a ring was recently removed. Vishvaksena's hand enters the frame from above, not touching — his index finger points at the gap from a careful distance. Oil lamp light from the left creates heavy shadows in the knuckle creases. Three planes: foreground Vishvaksena's pointing hand, middle the dead hand with its missing ring, background the stone floor falling into shadow.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "Every governor carried a signet ring. It sealed their charters. Authorized their commands. Made their word into law. Rajamalla's ring was gone. Whoever took it did not take the gold at his neck. This was not robbery. This was authority, transferred."

*Sound:* A low pulse — heartbeat-like, subtle. Stone ambiance.

**Purpose:** Second clue escalation. The missing signet ring shifts the crime from murder to political act. "Authority, transferred" — the narration names the stakes.

**Prompt notes:** The hands must look correct — the dead hand should be slightly discolored but not grotesque (this is hours after death in extreme heat, but we are not going for horror). The pale band where the ring was is the key detail — it must be clearly visible. Vishvaksena's pointing hand should show his sacred thread looping over it. The gold jewelry on the corpse should be Rashtrakuta-period: heavy, geometric, not filigree. NO touching between the hands — the pollution boundary is absolute.

## Scene 10 — The Archives

**Timestamp:** 3:30–4:30
**Duration:** 60s
**Beat:** The Turn

**Visual description:**
Interior: the akshapatala (records archive). A long, low-ceilinged room. Rows of wooden shelving filled with stacked copper plates, palm-leaf manuscripts wrapped in cloth, and sealed document bundles. Vishvaksena sits cross-legged on a low wooden platform, a copper plate in his hands, tilted to catch the light of an oil lamp beside him. More plates are spread around him on the platform. Single warm light source: the oil lamp. Deep shadows fill the room beyond. Three planes: foreground, a stack of copper plates with their ring-binding visible, surface reflecting lamplight; middle, Vishvaksena bent over his work; background, the dark archive shelves fading into Lamp Black.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "He went where he always went when the living made no sense. To the archives. The akshapatala — the record house. Every land grant, every tax exemption, every appointment the Rashtrakutas had ever made. Written on copper. Sealed with the royal seal. Designed to last a thousand years. They lasted longer than that. We can still read them."

*Sound:* The creak of copper plates being handled. The soft thud of documents being set down. Intimate, close-mic ambiance.

**Purpose:** THE TURN. The investigation shifts from crime scene to documentary analysis — Vishvaksena's true domain. The narration's last two sentences break the fourth wall gently: "We can still read them." This connects the 9th-century story to the present-day viewer and the actual surviving Rashtrakuta copper plates.

**Prompt notes:** The archive must feel like a real working records room, not a fantasy library. Wooden shelves, not stone. The copper plates should be dark with patina, held together by copper rings — this is historically documented. Palm-leaf manuscripts in cloth wrapping. The oil lamp creates a warm, intimate pool of light — this is Vishvaksena's natural habitat. His posture should be focused, comfortable — he belongs here. The wooden platform should show wear. Dust motes in the lamplight.

## Scene 11 — Oblique Light

**Timestamp:** 4:30–5:00
**Duration:** 30s
**Beat:** Consequences (Part 1)

**Visual description:**
Extreme close-up: a copper plate surface, tilted at a steep angle. An oil lamp held to the side creates extreme raking light across the engraved text. Under this oblique illumination, the surface tells two stories: the current text, cleanly engraved, and beneath it — ghostly, faint — the shadows of previous text that was scraped away and overwritten. The erasure is visible as a slightly different surface texture, a flatter polish where the original chisel marks were ground down. Vishvaksena's thumb at the plate's edge. Palette: Chola Bronze and Lamp Black, with Turmeric Gold (#D4A017) highlights where the lamplight catches the copper.

**Audio:**
*Narration:* "He tilted the plate. Brought the lamp close. In raking light, copper remembers everything. Beneath the new text, the old text whispered. Someone had scraped Rajamalla's charter clean. Recut new words into the metal. Changed the beneficiary. Changed who received the land, the revenue, the power."

*Sound:* The scrape of metal on metal — a stylus on copper, ghostly, imagined. Then silence.

**Purpose:** The forensic reveal. This is real epigraphy — oblique light analysis is the actual method for detecting copper-plate forgery. The narration makes it visceral: "copper remembers everything."

**Prompt notes:** This is the hero image of the episode — the forensic moment. The oblique lighting effect must be convincing: show how raking light reveals surface irregularities invisible under direct light. The "ghost text" beneath the current text should be subtle — not cartoon-obvious, but visible as a texture difference. The copper surface should show tool marks. Use Turmeric Gold accent sparingly for the lamp highlights. This frame must work at thumbnail size — the tilted copper plate with a bright light source is the readable shape.

## Scene 13 — The Seal

**Timestamp:** 5:30–6:30
**Duration:** 60s
**Beat:** Escalation

**Visual description:**
Two images in this scene.

**13A (5:30–6:00):** Close-up of the forged charter's seal — a copper ring binding the plates together, sealed with the Garuda (eagle-man) royal seal of the Rashtrakutas. The seal is intact. But the ring shows marks — it has been opened and re-closed. Fine scratches around the join that would not exist on an untouched seal. Oil lamp light. The Garuda seal is beautifully detailed: wings spread, humanoid body in a dynamic pose — Rashtrakuta royal iconography.

**13B (6:00–6:30):** Medium-wide shot: Vishvaksena stands in the archive, holding the forged charter in one hand, the oil lamp in the other. Around him, the shelves of records. He is looking toward the door — toward the world outside, toward the court, the palace, the boy-king. His body is half-turned, caught between the document and the door. The lamplight makes his shadow enormous on the wall behind him — the shadow of a single man stretched to fill the archive. Three planes: foreground, scattered plates on the platform; middle, Vishvaksena between lamp and door; background, the doorway with faint dawn light beyond.

**Audio:**
*Narration (over 13A):* "The royal seal was intact. Garuda, the eagle. The Rashtrakuta mark. But the binding ring had been opened. Whoever forged this charter had access to the royal seal. That narrowed the list. To a very short list."

*Narration (over 13B):* "Vishvaksena looked at the door. Beyond it was the court. The men who had served Govinda. The men who now circled his son. One of them had killed a governor, stolen his seal, forged a charter, and rearranged the revenue of an entire province. This was not a murder. This was a coup, written in copper."

*Sound:* Building tension — low strings, a pulse. The archive sounds have gone. Replaced by something larger, political.

**Purpose:** Escalation from forensic discovery to political crisis. "A coup, written in copper" — the episode's thesis statement. The shadow shot in 13B is the key visual metaphor: one man, carrying an enormous secret.

**Prompt notes:** 13A: The Garuda seal must be period-appropriate — Rashtrakuta Garuda iconography, not later Hoysala or Vijayanagara style. The scratches on the ring should be subtle, forensic. 13B: The shadow metaphor is critical — Vishvaksena's lamp-cast shadow should be dramatically large on the archive wall. The doorway light should be warm dawn light, not cool. His body language should convey weight — he is holding something dangerous. The scattered plates suggest hours of work.

## Scene 16 — The Cliffhanger
